# Sales test (Sales)

A training page for the recruiter. In the Performia / HR Scanner lectures there is **no separate "sales" test** — sales ability is assessed through productivity (the "Result" test) and through specific indicators of the "Tools" personality test. Below is what is actually present in the materials about salespeople and "doers", supplemented with the general methodology for assessing salespeople.

---

## What the sales assessment measures

Sales is an activity where three things matter: **drive and closing the deal, communication, and the ability to see the client's point of view without giving in to them at the expense of the work**. In the methodology, these abilities are not combined into a single separate "sales test" but are broken down into clear indicators.

### 1. Productivity — the foundation of any salesperson
The first and main filter is the **"Result"** test and the productivity interview. A salesperson is assessed by their past **results**: how much they sold, in what figures, whether the volume grew. As the lecture puts it directly: in an ordinary résumé there's a "hole" — duties are described, but it doesn't say "how much they sold". The "Result" test produces a **new résumé in the format of results** and assigns the candidate to a type: **Winner (performer) / Doer / Waiter**.

The key idea about sales: candidates apply en masse to anything with the word "sales" in it, without even reading the vacancy, and **every most ineffective person considers themselves an "active, positive, communicative salesperson"**. That is why you must rely on solid past results, not on self-assessment.

### 2. The salesperson's personality indicators (the "Tools" test)
The "Tools" test measures the effectiveness of the personality across 10 indicators (points A–J). The most significant for sales are:

- **Point F — Persistence.** In the materials it is called the **salesperson's primary tool**. High F: assertive, acts directly and simply, result-oriented, closes the deal, likes to give orders. Low F: "can't take a hit", acts cautiously, "around the corner", weakly result-oriented.
- **Point J — Sociability.** High J is needed by a salesperson: they initiate communication themselves, quickly establish contact, promote themselves, engage others. Low J (being withdrawn) is a minus for sales.
- **Point I — Sensitivity.** A subtle point: **too high an I is dangerous for a salesperson.** Such a person constantly says "yes", gives excessive discounts, yields to the client's manipulation at the company's expense. Moderate sensitivity is a plus (they sense the client), excessive is a risk.
- **Point B — Positivity** and **Point E — Activity** help: the salesperson sees solutions rather than obstacles and takes on many tasks.

### 3. An important warning from the materials
> "Do not cut off effective people if Tools seems suboptimal."

A direct quote about sales: you **cannot** tell a candidate "you have a low level of persistence, so you won't be able to work in sales" — this is called a **false idea**. If a person has **real results** in sales, then their set of tools allows them to be effective, no matter how "suboptimal" the Tools chart may look. Results matter more than the personality test.

---

## How the assessment proceeds

The assessment of a salesperson in the system follows the same funnel as any hire, but with an emphasis on sales:

1. The recruiter sends the candidate an email link to the **"Result"** test (20 open-ended questions, ~10–15 minutes). This is a filter for effectiveness and adequacy.
2. Based on the result — the type is determined (Winner / Doer / Waiter) and a résumé in the format of results is formed.
3. Suitable candidates are sent the **"Tools"** test (200 questions) — to see strengths and weaknesses and understand how to manage the person more effectively. Two tests are not sent at once (overload → the candidate "drops off").
4. If needed — an interview where, across 2–4 workplaces, real sales results are clarified.

A separate "Sales test" with its own questions and its own scale is not described in the materials.

---

## How to read the result

- **The productivity type** — the main thing. The ideal salesperson for complex sales is a **Winner (performer)**: sees and measures the product ("money in the till" + a satisfied client), compares their results with others, takes responsibility, works without a "kick". A **Doer** — a good performer under direction, useful for simple/scripted sales. A **Waiter** — not suited for sales.
- **The "Tools" profile** — is read like an "operating manual": high F and J are a good sign; check whether I is not too high (risk of excessive concessions); low persistence when real results are present — not grounds for rejection.
- **Talent for sales** is verified only by **past success** in sales — especially if selling with weak marketing support lies ahead.

---

## For which vacancies and why

The sales assessment is applied for positions: **sales manager, salesperson, negotiation specialist, account manager**, and similar roles, where the result is closed deals and money in the till.

Why it is needed:
- to screen out the large flow of self-proclaimed "ideal salespeople" and rely on real figures;
- to distinguish a born effective salesperson (Winner) from a performer (Doer) who needs a strong manager;
- to see in advance the risks of character (for example, excessive sensitivity → unnecessary discounts; low persistence → doesn't close deals) and understand how to manage this person.

**The salesperson's VFP** per the methodology: "money in the till" (and, for work with regular clients — also a satisfied client who will return).
