# Logic and quick-wittedness test (IQ)

A training page for the recruiter. The material is based on Performia / HR Scanner lectures ("Tests and personality traits", the "Result" test) with the addition of the general methodology of intelligence testing.

---

## What this test is and what it measures

The system has a separate **intelligence test (IQ)** — the simplest and shortest of all the tests. Its task is to show **how well and how quickly a person thinks**. To put it more precisely, the test measures **a person's ability to formulate, analyze, and solve problems**.

The materials state plainly: this is a common tool found in many systems, and it is not a "source of pride" for the developers — of a large seminar only about 10 minutes are devoted to it. Nevertheless, for a number of roles it is useful.

**The definition of intelligence that underlies the test** (also used in the "Result" test):

> Intelligence — is the ability **to see differences in things that are alike, and to find similarities in things that are different**.

An example from the lecture: two tangerines seem identical, but one is more to the left, the other to the right, one is bigger — a person with good intelligence notices differences where at first glance everything is alike. And conversely: a glass and a tangerine are different objects, but both contain liquid, both have common elements — an intelligent person sees this similarity.

Important: the word "Logic"/"logic test" as a separate name does **not appear** in the materials. What can be called "logic" is represented in the system precisely by the **intelligence test (IQ)** plus a built-in logical task in the first question of the "Result" test (whether the candidate can draw an analogy: a janitor's product — a clean yard — and apply it to their own role).

---

## How the test proceeds

- A link to the test is sent to the candidate by email (the recruiter clicks a button in the dashboard — there's no need to meet the candidate in person).
- The candidate answers **multiple-choice** questions in an online form.
- Intelligence tests traditionally have a **time limit** — both correctness and speed are measured, which is why "thinks quickly" is part of the measured quality.
- The result automatically arrives in the recruiter's dashboard: the system produces a **numerical indicator — the intelligence quotient (IQ)** — and also produces a **gradation** (a verbal assessment of the level).

---

## How to read the result: the IQ gradation

The system produces a number and assigns it to a level. The materials provide this scale with the share of the population at each level:

| IQ score | Level assessment | Approximate share of population |
|---|---|---|
| **below 80** | very low | ~5% |
| **80–89** | low | ~10% |
| **90–99** | below average | ~35% |
| **100–109** | slightly above average (100 ≈ average) | ~35% |
| **110–134** | high | ~10% |
| **135 and above** | very high | (the remainder) |

A benchmark: **100 is the average intelligence quotient**. There are roughly as many very intelligent people as there are very "weak" ones on this indicator (the scale is more or less symmetrical).

When reading the result, the recruiter looks both at the number itself and at which zone it falls into. For most roles an average level is sufficient; certain roles require a high one.

## Why this test is needed in hiring

The IQ test is needed where the work involves **analysis, reasoning, comprehension, working through complex situations**: analytics, working with data, tasks where you need to "think well and quickly". For such vacancies it is a valuable additional filter.

At the same time, the lecture firmly warns about **two limitations** of the test — they must be kept in mind:

1. **IQ does not show in which direction a person thinks well.** High intelligence in itself does not guarantee that a person will direct it to the benefit of the work.
2. **IQ does not directly show productivity.** There are people with very high intelligence and weak productivity — and, conversely, very effective people who "look a bit dim".

Hence the conclusion repeated in the materials: **the intelligence test is an additional factor, not the main one.** The view "we only need intelligent employees" is called **dangerous**. In some companies IQ is the only thing checked in candidates, and this is a mistake: the main hiring factor remains **productivity** (see the "Rules for working with the system" page), while IQ merely completes the picture.

---

## The link to learnability

The ability to quickly formulate, analyze, and solve problems is directly linked to **how quickly a person masters new things**. Higher intelligence usually means the candidate will figure out an unfamiliar task faster, and get through onboarding and training faster. This makes IQ a useful benchmark precisely for positions with a high barrier to entry and a large volume of new material.

But learnability is not the only or the decisive factor. According to the methodology, knowledge ranks last among the hiring factors ("knowledge can be given"), and the mere fact of quick thinking does not yet make a person effective. That is why the correct logic is: **first — productivity and personality traits, and only then IQ as a clarifying touch**, especially for analytical and intellectually demanding roles.

---

## Practical rules for the recruiter

- Send the IQ test selectively — where the work really requires "thinking quickly".
- Do not screen out an effective candidate merely because of an average IQ if their past results show they are successful in their field.
- Do not hire on a high IQ alone: it guarantees neither benefit nor results.
- Always cross-reference IQ with productivity (the "Result" test) and personality traits (the "Tools" test).
