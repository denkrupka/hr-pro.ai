# Personality traits test "Tools" (Tools / Exec-U-Test)

Training material for the recruiter. Here we cover what the "Tools" test is, how it is structured, how to read the chart of 10 points, and how to use the result in hiring.

---

## 1. What this test is and what it measures

"Tools" is the HR portal's personality test, the second in order after the "Result" productivity test. It is based on the **Oxford Capacity Analysis** (OCA), also known as the **Exec-U-Test**. The name itself stands for **Exec** (executive) + **U** (understanding) + **Test**, that is, "a test that helps an executive understand a subordinate". The methodology is more than 50 years old.

It is important to correctly understand exactly what the test measures. It shows not "whether a person is good or bad" but the **set of tools** (tools in English — hence the name) that a person uses to achieve results. The test assesses **the effectiveness of the personality across 10 indicators** — across 10 "points". In essence it is a **photograph of the personality at a given moment in time**: the indicators change depending on mood and life situation, and if the test is taken again a month later, the values will be somewhat different.

The key point for the recruiter: personality traits are a **negative indicator**. The best result is "zero", that is, the absence of contraindications. We do **not** select a candidate by personality traits — we select by productivity, and we use the "Tools" test to assess **risk**: how much the character will get in the way in a specific role, how much it will get in the way within the team, and how to manage such a person more effectively (a kind of "operating manual").

### Structure of the test
- **200 questions** — these are 10 small tests, shuffled together, with **20 questions per point**.
- **Three answer options:** "Yes" (plus, `+`), "Maybe / sometimes" (middle, `M`), and "No" (minus, `–`). Plus means mostly "yes" or a firm "yes"; the middle means uncertainty, not settled as "yes" or "no"; minus means mostly "no".
- Answers should be given **about the present time**: if the answer differs for the past and the present, the candidate goes by how things are now.

---

## 2. How the candidate takes the test

A link arrives in the candidate's email, they open the test online and answer all 200 questions in sequence. The rules for filling it in are simple: don't dwell too long on a single question — answer immediately, as soon as the question is understood, and move on to the next one; answer **every** question; read a question as many times as needed to understand it.

Before taking the test, the candidate fills in a questionnaire: first name, last name, age, phone, and mandatorily **sex**. This is not a formality: **results are normalized by sex and age**. The same set of answers from a man and a woman (or a teenager) will produce different charts, because each group has its own normalization tables. Age groups: under 14, 14–30, and over 30.

The order in the hiring funnel matters: first the candidate takes **"Result"** (the productivity test), and only then — "Tools". Personality cannot be assessed in isolation from results: if a person has no results, they may be very pleasant but incapable of achieving a goal. And conversely — **do not "cut off" effective candidates just because "Tools" seems suboptimal**.

---

## 3. The chart of 10 points: scale and zones

The test result is a **vertical chart of 10 points, A–J**. Each point is a separate personality trait, and each point has its own value.

### The scale
Each point's scale runs **from −100 to +100**. The zero line is the central axis of the chart. A point's value is its coordinate on this scale (for example, −95, −8, +34, +90, +96).

How the value is derived: from the 20 answers for each point a "raw score" is calculated (each "−/M/+" answer is assigned its own weight), then this raw score is converted into signed percentages from −100 to +100 using the normalization table selected by the candidate's sex and age. The result is 10 values, and these are the "10 points of the chart".

### Interpretation zones
Each point falls into one of **five zones**, and each zone has its own color:

| Zone | Color | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| Very low | red | critically low trait |
| Low | orange | reduced trait |
| Medium | yellow | normal |
| High | green | pronounced trait |
| Very high | light blue | very strongly pronounced trait |

An important subtlety: **the boundaries of the zones differ across points** (and depend on sex/age). That is, the same number gives a different zone for different points. For example, +20 for point A is the low range, for point B it is already very high, and for point E it is the boundary between very low and low. This is not a single linear grid but a normalized profile. For point B (positivity) and E (activity), **zero** is the boundary between positive and negative.

## 4. Analysis of the 10 points A–J

The points are logically grouped into three blocks plus one special point:

- **BE (A, B, C)** — inner qualities, self-perception, comfort with oneself ("how much space a person has").
- **D — certainty** stands apart (light blue color), it is the reliability of the whole test.
- **DO (E, F, G)** — energy, action, effectiveness ("how much energy a person has").
- **HAVE (H, I, J)** — relationships with people, the ability to "possess" ("how much time a person has").

### A — Attentiveness (the "Be" block)
Measures the ability to concentrate and focus attention, the striving for planning, order, precision.
- **High score:** the person concentrates well, plans, systematizes (lists, schedules), keeps order, notices details and facial expressions, can wait, is able to look into the future (organize a project, open a branch).
- **Low score:** scattered, dislikes planning, a weak organizer, cannot wait, untidy, does not notice details. In the middle zone, attention is enough only for the current tasks but not for planning the future.
- **Compulsive A** (very high, answered "yes" to all 20 questions): cannot NOT plan, rigid, less flexible, with fixed ideas (a "pedant").
- **Roles:** high — executive, lawyer, accountant, watchman. Not critical — loader, creative professions.

### B — Positivity (the "Be" block)
Measures the chronic mood level: whether the person is solution-oriented or depressed. It is an indirect **honesty indicator** (happy people are less often dishonest). The boundary is **zero**: above = positive, below = negative.
- **High (>0):** sees solutions, open, honest, likes simplicity, often wins (believes and tries), strong in strategy (sees the whole picture), creative, smiles.
- **Low:** negative, sees problems and obstacles, easily distorts the truth, delivers only bad news and blows it out of proportion, often loses, weak in strategy.
- **Floating B** (a "lightning" symbol, red): mood floats like a "roller coaster". A sign of suppression. In itself, not a stop factor.
- **Compulsive B:** cannot NOT rejoice, instead of a solution puts things off ("everything will be fine"), reports that all is excellent even in the face of failure.
- **Roles:** high is important for reception, hostess, nanny.

### C — Composure (the "Be" block)
Measures effectiveness under stress, control of emotions and the body.
- **High:** calm, controls emotions and body, effective under load, hard to unsettle, does not blow up, falls asleep easily, knows how to relax (an example of very high — a tightrope walker).
- **Low:** nervous, fidgety, fine motor habits (fiddles with nose, ear, hair), explosive, easily upset, sleeps lightly, does not relax.
- **Compulsive C:** cannot NOT control, hides feelings, seeks to command; in a very difficult situation the control can abruptly disappear.
- **Roles:** high — nanny, watchman, stressful work.

### D — Certainty (stands apart, light blue color)
**The most important point for assessing the reliability of the test.** It is the only point with a distinct color. High D → the test can be trusted; low or below average → "tomorrow they'll change their mind and flip", the indicators cannot be trusted.
Measures the level of predictability, reliability, certainty; conservatism versus a tendency toward change and risk.
- **KEY THRESHOLD: +32.** Above 32 — "likes to find out before doing" (conservative, reliable, predictable, copes with routine, needs a plan). Below 32 — "likes to try before finding out" (impulsive, flexible, likes risk and change, dislikes routine, unreliable). A value of 34 is already high (the threshold is passed); −8 is below average, cannot be trusted.
- **Compulsive D:** sticks out at least 25–30 points above all the other points (in the very high zone). Cannot act unless certain; the "obliged to be right" syndrome. Suits a chief executive; for a commercial director such a profile is a burden.
- **Important:** D is raised by learning — the more a person knows in a field, the more predictably they act within it.

### E — Activity (the "Do" block)
Measures the level of energy and vital forces. The boundary is **zero** (below = little energy).
- **High:** energetic, likes movement, starts things, takes initiative, interested in the new, has much stamina, takes on many tasks.
- **Low (<0):** passive, low energy, dislikes starting anything new, little initiative, acts only in familiar areas.
- **Compulsive E** (an "Energizer"): cannot NOT do, cannot sit still, quickly gets bored without something new, may break an already working scheme (cannot help changing things).
- **Floating E** (a "lightning"): energy is variable (there's a desire — there's no desire). A sign of suppression.
- **Roles:** high — where you need to start and move; low — routine.

### F — Persistence (the "Do" block)
Measures the ability to bring things to completion, drive. **Directly linked to result-orientation.**
- **High:** assertive, acts directly and simply, result-oriented, sees things through, learns quickly, likes to give orders. The salesperson's primary tool.
- **Low:** "can't take a hit", cautious, acts "from a distance, around the corner", not direct, weakly result-oriented, learns slowly.
- **Compulsive F:** applies more force than needed — breaks things, "burns out clients"; pursues the goal even when it is already irrelevant.
- **Roles:** salespeople, executives.

### G — Responsibility (the "Do" block)
Measures whether a person takes on responsibility and likes being held accountable; whether they can accept criticism. This is **not about irresponsibility** but about the willingness to **answer to others**.
- **High:** easily holds up under pressure, calmly accepts and resolves criticism, sees consequences, quickly gives feedback, deserves trust. A medium level is already considered good.
- **Low:** vulnerable to criticism, touchy, avoids pressure, shuts down, poorly sees consequences; prefers to do things themselves rather than answer to someone.
- **Compulsive G** (a "chess player"): calculates what to say and do for the result, builds combinations, treats people like chess pieces.

### H — Objectivity (the "Have" block)
Measures fairness and diplomacy: whether the person is oriented toward agreement and solution — or argues and looks for someone to blame.
- **High:** fair, oriented toward agreement and solution, diplomatic, focuses on the positives, builds long-term relationships, strong in tactics, prioritizes well. Medium is already good.
- **Low:** critical, points out mistakes ("you're wrong here"), undiplomatic, spoils relationships over differences of opinion, suspicious, prone to accusations.
- **Compulsive H:** explains and argues everything → unproductive discussions, stands their ground to the end.
- **Roles (high):** judge, lawyer, diplomat, PR director, brand manager, HR director.

### I — Sensitivity (the "Have" block)
Measures warmth and sensitivity: whether other people's feelings and comfort matter to the person, whether they see another's point of view.
- **High:** warm-hearted, likes to help, easily shows feelings, thanks sincerely, forms close relationships, notices the feelings and positions of others.
- **Low:** cold, closed off, keeps feelings to themselves, egoistic, rarely thanks, keeps their distance, sees only their own point of view.
- **Compulsive I:** always says "yes" to requests at the expense of their own work, forgets their own point of view, treats themselves as nothing. Risk of manipulation (the "doormat", "rose-colored glasses" syndromes).
- **Roles:** high — nanny, office manager; dangerous — salesperson (excessive discounts), reception; low is appropriate — police, military.

### J — Sociability (the "Have" block)
Measures whether a person likes communication, seeks out contacts and attention — or is withdrawn.
- **High:** vivid, initiates communication themselves, quickly establishes contact, likes being the center of attention, promotes themselves, is easy to understand.
- **Low:** withdrawn, rarely goes first, establishes contact slowly and with strain, dislikes new acquaintances, has difficulty conveying thoughts (an example — a programmer with sociability of −90, a "god of programming").
- **Compulsive J:** cannot stay silent, talks more than they listen, about themselves and their achievements.
- **Roles:** high — sales, HR manager, recruiter, host; low is appropriate — programmer, analyst, lathe operator.

## 5. Syndromes, imbalances, and "manias"

Individual points are only half the picture. The main thing in interpretation is the **combinations and skews** between the points.

### Syndromes (combinations of points)
A syndrome is a strongly pronounced effect arising from a certain combination or skew of points. Often a syndrome is defined not by an absolute value but by the **difference between points**. The only explicit numeric threshold for a difference is "significantly, by at least 20 points" (for comparing D and E). Examples of syndromes from the materials:

- **A, B, C low** → neurotic, burdened by past losses, possible neglect in childhood.
- **Low A, C, D, G, H, J** → a bad worker.
- **C and H low** → problems in the present.
- **D, G, H, J low** → denies existence.
- **E higher than F** → does more than they could without strain; **F higher than E** → "doesn't do what they could" (lazy).
- **E > D by at least 20 points** → likes the new, better at starting than at working in routine; **D > E** → a conservative, for routine work.
- **E and F low** → a tendency toward alcoholism or drug abuse (if the rest of the chart is acceptable).
- **D low, J high** → unrestrained, compulsive.
- **D low, G high** → a forced extrovert.
- **E, F, J low** → possible hormonal deficiency (worth consulting a doctor).
- **F high, G and H low** → a difficult person.
- **High I, J, B with low/medium F** → pleasant, easy to get along with.
- **I high, J low** → a "teddy bear" (goodwill, people take advantage of it).
- **D — the highest, above all others** → "obliged to be right".

The system itself calculates and displays the syndromes. The recruiter's task is to understand their meaning and take them into account when selecting for a specific role.

### "Manias" and floating points ("lightnings")
Two points can be marked with a **wavy line** (a "lightning" symbol) — these are **B (Positivity)** and **E (Activity)**. A wavy line means the level of the trait is **unstable**, floating:
- **Floating B** is assigned if the question about periods of depression and sadness without an apparent cause was answered "Yes". The level of happiness jumps: periods of general cheerfulness give way to depression.
- **Floating E** is assigned if the question about periods of heightened activity for several days in a row was answered "Yes". Periods of activity give way to passivity.

Floating points are a **sign of suppression**: there is a suppressive personality near the person (about 3% of the population). In themselves they are not stop factors. But if both points float at the same time (**B + E = the PTP syndrome**, a Potential Trouble source), that is already a serious signal: a "roller coaster", the person attracts trouble, often falls ill, gets into accidents, or becomes a victim of theft at a critical moment. Such a person is effective only when fully loaded with work.

## 6. How to read the result as a whole

There is a clear order for reading the chart:

1. **First we look at point D (Certainty).** This is the reliability of the whole test. If D is high — the test can be trusted. If it is low or below average (the threshold is +32) — the person is unstable in their decisions, and the other indicators cannot be trusted. This is the first thing an experienced assessor looks at.
2. **We read by blocks:** "Be" (A, B, C) → "Do" (E, F, G) → "Have" (H, I, J). If the first three points are low (or two of the three) — the person has problems with exchange (usually a shortage of outflow).
3. **We look at both the absolute values and the difference between points.** A skew between points = a syndrome.
4. **We note the compulsive points** (sticking out 25–30 points above the others in the very high zone) — this is a separate emphasis.
5. **We check the "lightnings"** on B and E (manias) and the PTP syndrome.

Separately, the system guards against unreliable completion: if a person answered at random (did not understand the questions or was inattentive), all 10 points fall into a narrow, predictable matrix of ranges — this is a "random chart", it cannot be interpreted and means the test is unreliable.

When presenting the assessment to the candidate themselves, the rule applies: **first we talk at length about the positives** (of both high and low points), and only then, once the person has begun to trust — about the negatives. Whoever jabs at the negatives is perceived as an enemy, and their help is not accepted; whoever looks at the positives is a friend.

---

## 7. Why the test is accurate and why it is needed in hiring

The test's accuracy is ensured by several mechanisms:
- **200 questions, 20 per point** — each trait is measured many times from different angles, random answers average out.
- **Three answer options** (yes / maybe / no) give a finer gradation than "yes/no".
- **Normalization by sex and age** — the result is compared not with an abstract norm but with the relevant group.
- **The unreliability detector** (a "random chart") screens out those who answered at random.
- **Point D as a reliability indicator** — the methodology itself indicates whether a particular result can be trusted.
- **Manias and syndromes** catch the instability and dangerous combinations that are not visible in individual points.

In hiring, the "Tools" test is the **second factor** after productivity. Its role is not selection but the **assessment of risk** and the drafting of an employee's "operating manual". It answers three questions: how much the character will get in the way in this role, how much it will get in the way within the team, and how to manage this person more effectively. This is precisely why "Tools" is sent only to those who have already passed the productivity filter ("Result"): personality is assessed only in those capable of delivering results. And the recruiter's main rule remains unchanged — **we hire based on productivity, and based on personal qualities we assess risk rather than filter out effective people.**
