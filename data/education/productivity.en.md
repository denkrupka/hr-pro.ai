# Productivity test ("Result" / Result)

A training page for the recruiter. The material is based on Performia / HR Scanner lectures (the "Result" test, the sessions "Productivity", "Questions for performers", "Questions for doers", "Productivity and exchange").

---

## What this test is and what it measures

**"Result"** (from the word "result") is the first and most important test in the system. Its name points directly to its essence: the test looks at the **product, the final result** of a person's work. The task of the test, as stated in the lecture, is:

> The task of the "Result" test is **to assess the candidate's results and adequacy.**

Why results specifically? Because classic recruiting relies on a résumé that the candidate composed themselves in order to appeal to the employer. Such a résumé lists positions and duties, but has almost no results: how much the person sold, whether they grew, whether they are better than the rest or worse. "Result" extracts precisely this missing information and, as output, produces a **new résumé in the format of results**.

At the same time, the test works as a **rough adequacy filter**. The very fact that the candidate opened the email and filled in the questionnaire already makes them motivated. And the answers to the open-ended questions quickly show whether it is worth talking to the person at all.

The key principle on which the test's accuracy rests:

> Effectiveness is an **innate** quality. A person is either born effective or not. That is why we analyze the **past**: if a person delivered benefit before, they will most likely deliver it for us too. If there were no results in the past — we have no grounds to hope that the moment of getting the job will turn out to be a turning point in their fate.

Hence the definition that lies at the core of the test:

> **Productivity** — a characteristic (quality) of a person indicating the presence of benefit, that is, results, at work and/or in life — in the present and/or in the past.

## How the candidate takes the test

- In the dashboard the recruiter enters the candidate's email, selects the vacancy, and clicks "Send the 'Result' test". There is no need to meet in person.
- The candidate receives an email: "So that the upcoming interview is as effective as possible, please answer a short questionnaire about your results at previous jobs. This will take no more than 10 minutes." The email not only sends the test but also **sells** participation.
- The candidate follows the link, enters their first name, last name, age, phone, sex — and answers **20 questions**.
- **All questions are open-ended**: each requires an explanation in the candidate's own words. This is important — otherwise the candidate would "hide" behind socially correct options, and we wouldn't see the truth.
- The test is **non-linear**: if a question is answered "no" (for example, "I don't measure the volume of the product"), then the follow-up question is simply not asked.

**Timing.** The candidate spends 10–15 minutes. The recruiter spends about **1 minute** assessing the result, and sometimes 10–15 seconds is enough to understand whether it's worth moving on. This is precisely where the saving lies: from ~100 applications the test narrows the flow down to ~20 people worth talking to, of whom 1–3 are hired.

## Three categories by result: Winner, Doer, Waiter

The main outcome of the test is assigning the candidate to one of three categories. In the HR Scanner materials they are called by English terms; in Performia training the first two categories are called "performer" and "doer".

### Winner / performer

A person who is inherently effective. Their distinctive trait is that they **see and can FORMULATE the end product (VFP) of any role**. Whatever they do, they always look at what should result. A Winner measures their product, compares results with others, takes responsibility, is proactive, and works autonomously — without a "kick".

There are two subtypes:
- **Winner-generalist** — effective in all areas: at work, in sport, and in the family.
- **Winner-specialist** — effective in one narrow area, and in others may feel uncertain.

> **A crucial clarification.** A performer differs from a doer NOT in that one produces a product and the other does not. Both are productive and produce a product. The difference is that a performer **sees and formulates** the product, whereas a doer produces it but may not see it. A common mistake is to think that "a performer = someone who produces a product". This is incorrect: a performer is someone who sees it.

### Doer

A good performer with a strong desire to work. And desire is the foundation of ability. A doer is not afraid of responsibility and likes there to be plenty of work. They produce results but **do not formulate the end product** — instead they list their actions, duties, and processes ("I held 15 meetings", "I made 50 calls").

A doer works excellently **under a Winner's direction**. This, in the lecturer's words, is "one of the most useful employees", and there are many times more doers than performers. A sure sign of a good doer is that their **circle of duties and area of responsibility constantly grows**: the rule "we ride on whoever carries us" is at work — new tasks are entrusted to the most loaded and reliable person.

### Waiter (the one who waits)

A person who **dislikes work** and additional tasks and **is afraid of responsibility**. For them, the volume of work and responsibility does not grow over time (and may even decline), there are no results, and to questions they "waffle" — explaining why in their case the product cannot be named, measured, or compared. Failing the adequacy check is a typical Waiter signal.

### How many there are in the population

Approximately **20% winners, 40% doers, 40% waiters**. The recruiter's task is to find those 20% of winners and complement them with the 40% of good doers.

## What "the product of a role" is and why it is the core of the test

To read the answers, the recruiter themselves must firmly understand what a product is. The definition (per Hubbard) given in the lectures:

> **A product** — is a completed, high-quality service or article in the hands of the consumer as an exchange for something of value.

This definition has four conditions:
1. **Completed** — finished. A refrigerator with a door that won't close is not a product.
2. **High-quality** — meets the standards of its price category. Not "a Mercedes versus a Lada", but "runs versus doesn't run".
3. **In the hands of the consumer** — delivered and received. Refrigerators in the warehouse are not yet a product; the product appears when it has been delivered.
4. **As an exchange for something of value** — something is received in return: money, a signature on an acceptance form, and sometimes simply a nod of the head (a confirmation of understanding).

**The VFP (valuable final product) of a role** — is the final formulation of what is produced, not a list of minor tasks. Examples: a janitor — a clean yard; a secretary — a call quickly routed to the right employee; a driver — a passenger or cargo delivered on time; a salesperson — "money in the till" (plus a satisfied client, if the store relies on repeat customers); a personal assistant — the manager's time and attention saved; the product of communication — understanding. A correctly formulated product can always be measured.

**The law of exchange** helps understand the candidate's motivation. Exchange comes in four kinds: with surplus (you give 110, receive 100 — the most valuable), fair (100 for 100), unfair (you receive the full amount, give less), and criminal (you take without giving). At the foundation of any exchange lies the **outflow**: to receive something, you must first give something. Valuable candidates are those oriented toward what they give, not only toward what they receive.

## How to read the answers: indicators by question

The recruiter mentally marks each answer with a "plus" (toward Winner) or a "minus". Several "minuses" in a row are grounds for rejection. Here are the key questions and what the answers mean.

**"Do you agree that every role has a product?"** (with the janitor example). This is a built-in intelligence check — whether the person is able to draw an analogy to their own work.
- "Yes, I agree" with a meaningful explanation — a plus.
- "No, there are roles without a product" (as if an accountant or a CEO has no product) — the first warning sign of inadequacy.

**"How would you describe your product at your last job?"** — the most important indicator question.
- Names a real end product ("my product is an effectively hired employee who started work and brings profit") — a **Winner**.
- "It's hard to describe, I can list duties" and lists actions — a **Doer**.
- "In this job it's impossible to name a product" — a **Waiter**, the answer of very weak people.

**"Do you measure the volume of the product you produce?"** A Winner measures almost always. "Yes" + precise figures — a plus. "No" — a minus (and the follow-up question is skipped).

**"Describe your exact results over a period."**
- Precise figures ("10 deals worth N million", "20 employees a month, none quit") — a Winner.
- "Roughly", processes ("600 calls", "1000 résumés") — a Doer.
- "Impossible to measure" with an explanation of why — a Waiter, "waffling". Important: the size of the result is not critical, what is critical is the ability to talk about it.

**"Can you compare your results with your colleagues?"** A Winner gives an assessment: "higher / about the same / a little lower". "It's hard for me to compare" — a minus.

**"How did the volume of work and the volume of responsibility change?"** Growth — likes work, is trusted (Winner/Doer). Doesn't grow or declines — a Waiter (not ready to take things on, isn't trusted).

**"Do you act according to plans? Are results above or below plan?"** There is no correct answer — this is an adequacy check. A reasoned answer (I exceed them; or I perform better than everyone even though the plans are inflated) — a plus. "Idiots set the plans" — blatant inadequacy, the candidate is unacceptable.

**"Whom did you report to? Name and position."** A willingness to give a contact (sometimes immediately a phone number for verification) — openness, a plus. "That's a corporate secret" — a warning, possible deception.

**"What motivates you?" and "What is important for you to be happy at work?"** If in both places, and in the exchange formula, the answer is "only money" — that's the weakest motivation (such a person will leave for a raise of a thousand rubles). Development, values, contribution, recognition — a plus.

**"Give examples of what you're proud of — at work and in life, sport, studies, family."** Shows breadth. Achievements in different areas — a Winner-generalist; in one — a Winner-specialist; emptiness or "I haven't achieved anything" — failed the adequacy check.

## How to conduct the assessment correctly (steps of a live interview)

A live productivity interview (5–10 minutes) repeats the logic of the test.

1. **Warn that the interview will be short** — otherwise the candidate, out of habit, "dozes" through the first 10 minutes. Plus it's a matter of PR: many people pass through the recruiter, and all of them form an opinion of the company.
2. **Define the word "product"** — a couple of sentences and, mandatorily, an example (a janitor — a clean yard). It is almost impossible to lead a doer to a performer's answer, so there's no need to fear the example.
3. **Ask the main question: "What was your product?"** They formulate the product — we move on. They list actions — give a **second chance**, rephrasing the question ("what were you ultimately paid money for?"). They don't use the chance — move to the doer's interview (not "out the door").
4. **Check that the idea is truly theirs: "How did you measure it?"** Whoever sees the product naturally takes an interest in how much of it they produced.
5. **"How much did you produce?"** — the actual check of scale.

For a **doer**, the desire to work is determined through the growth of the circle of duties and responsibility — not by asking outright "were there additional duties?" (that's a prompt), but simply by inquiring about duties at the start and end of the job, comparing with colleagues.

Separately — **reference checking**: "Who can confirm your results?" The past is verifiable, the future is not.

## Why the test is accurate and why to use it

- **It looks to the past, not the future.** Résumés and "plans for the job" are self-presentation. The test asks about real past results that can be verified.
- **Productivity is innate and does not change** — past results reliably predict future ones.
- **Open-ended questions** don't allow hiding behind pretty phrasing.
- **A built-in check of adequacy and intelligence** screens out those who don't understand basic things.
- **An enormous saving of time**: from 100 applications — 20 conversations — 1–3 hires, and the assessment takes about a minute.
- **The priority of the factor.** Productivity is the main hiring factor: knowledge can be given, motivation raised a little, but productivity cannot. That is why the rule is categorical: if a person is not productive, they are of no interest under any circumstances.
